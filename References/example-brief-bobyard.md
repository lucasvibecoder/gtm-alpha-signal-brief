## 1. Executive Context

Bobyard is an AI-powered takeoff and estimating platform that uses proprietary computer vision to automate 70–90% of the quantity and material takeoff process for commercial landscape contractors. Founded in 2023 by Stanford CS graduate Michael Ding, the company raised a $35M Series A led by 8VC in December 2025 and serves mid-market and enterprise landscaping firms handling $3M–$20M+ commercial installation projects. Bobyard is executing a vertical AI playbook—win the hardest trade first (landscaping), then expand into drywall, electrical, HVAC, plumbing, and framing—in a $1T+ U.S. construction market that remains one of the least digitized industries on earth.

---

## 2. Value Proposition Analysis

**What they solve (real pain, not marketing language):**
Estimators at commercial landscape contractors spend hours to days manually counting thousands of plant symbols, measuring linear footage, and calculating area coverage from architectural drawings—where a single miscount can blow a bid margin or lose a project entirely. Bobyard's CV models automate symbol counting, linear measurement, area detection, and text parsing simultaneously, cutting takeoff time by 50–70% and enabling a single estimator to turn out $30M in bids instead of $15M.

**Who feels this pain most acutely:**
Commercial landscape contractors in the $5M–$50M revenue range who are capacity-constrained on estimating headcount. Specifically, firms where the owner or a single senior estimator is the bottleneck on bid volume—they're leaving revenue on the table because they physically cannot respond to enough RFPs. This pain spikes during bid season (spring) and when firms are crossing the residential-to-commercial threshold.

**The cost of inaction:**
- Estimating errors cost U.S. construction firms an estimated $273B annually (NCHRP data), with up to 20% of total project costs attributable to estimation mistakes
- A landscape contractor with one estimator capped at $15M in annual bids is forfeiting $15M+ in addressable work
- Manual inconsistency across estimators leads to unpredictable margins—winning unprofitable bids or losing profitable ones
- Competitors adopting AI takeoff tools are submitting 3–5x more bids per estimator, structurally outcompeting manual shops

**Their actual edge:**
Bobyard runs dozens of proprietary computer vision models built from scratch for construction drawings—not fine-tuned general models. First-run accuracy exceeds 95% with 90%+ automation of the takeoff task. They started with landscaping, the most visually complex trade (thousands of arbitrary symbols varying project to project), which means their CV pipeline is battle-tested on the hardest problem. Competitors like Togal.AI and Beam AI focus on general construction or aerial imagery; Bobyard owns the blueprint-to-bid workflow for landscaping specifically, with trade-specific templates, vendor quote databases, and assembly tracking built in.

## 7. Competitive Intelligence Summary

**Primary Competitors:**

1. **Beam AI (formerly Attentive.ai)** — Broadest trade coverage with 15+ trades, focuses on both aerial imagery takeoffs (field services) and blueprint takeoffs (construction). Trusted by 1,100+ businesses. Positions as the all-in-one AI takeoff platform.

2. **SiteRecon** — AI-driven landscape mapping and takeoff focused on aerial/satellite imagery for maintenance and snow removal. Trusted by 5 of top 10 LM150 companies. Stronger in property maintenance than construction installation.

3. **Togal.AI** — Cloud-based takeoff for general construction (not landscape-specific). 98% accuracy claim. Focuses on architectural plan analysis. Less depth in landscape-specific symbol recognition and plant species handling.

**Where Bobyard wins:**
- **Commercial landscape installation projects ($3M–$20M+)** where drawings contain thousands of trade-specific symbols, species, and materials. No competitor has Bobyard's depth of landscape-specific CV models.
- **Mid-market contractors ($5M–$30M revenue)** who need a tool purpose-built for their trade, not a generic construction platform. These firms find Togal too general and SiteRecon too focused on maintenance.
- **Speed + accuracy combination**: 95%+ first-run accuracy with 90%+ automation of the takeoff task. Competitors that cover 15+ trades sacrifice depth for breadth.

**Where Bobyard loses:**
- **Multi-trade general contractors** who need a single platform for electrical, mechanical, plumbing, and landscape. Beam AI's breadth wins here today (though Bobyard's expansion plans address this).
- **Maintenance-focused landscapers** who primarily do mowing, snow removal, and property upkeep. SiteRecon's aerial imagery approach is better suited for recurring maintenance pricing.
- **Price-sensitive small contractors (<$2M revenue)** who can't justify SaaS spend and handle bid volume manually. Bobyard's mid-market/enterprise positioning may price them out.

**Gaps in competitor GTM:**
- **No competitor is running signal-based outbound tied to job postings, PE acquisitions, or residential-to-commercial transitions.** The market defaults to trade show presence, SEO, and partner channel marketing. First-mover advantage on signal-triggered outbound is significant.
- **SiteRecon dominates the maintenance conversation but doesn't own the installation/construction narrative.** Bobyard can own "commercial landscape construction" as a category while SiteRecon owns "landscape maintenance."
- **None of the competitors are targeting PE-backed rollup platforms as a dedicated segment.** This is a blue ocean play for Bobyard given the 30+ active PE platforms in landscaping.

## 8. Strategic Recommendations

**Recommendation 1: Own the "Estimator Shortage" Narrative**

> **Do:** Build a content and outbound engine around the construction estimator shortage. Publish an annual "State of Landscape Estimating" report with real data (salary trends, unfilled positions, bid capacity gaps). Use this as the top-of-funnel magnet and the contextual frame for all outbound.
> **Instead of:** Generic product marketing about "AI-powered takeoff" that blends into every competitor's messaging.
> **Because:** 92% of contractors report hiring difficulty. 306,000 construction jobs are unfilled. This is the macro narrative that every landscape contractor is living. Bobyard's message should be "you don't have a hiring problem, you have a throughput problem"—and that reframe is what creates urgency for a software purchase instead of another Indeed posting.

**Recommendation 2: Build a Dedicated PE/Platform Sales Motion**

> **Do:** Create a dedicated outreach play targeting the 30+ PE-backed landscape platforms with a consultative, multi-entity value proposition. Build an ROI model that shows portfolio-wide impact. Partner with landscape-focused PE advisors (e.g., Livingstone Partners, who actively cover this space).
> **Instead of:** Treating PE-backed platforms the same as individual contractor accounts with the same sales motion and messaging.
> **Because:** PE platforms are the fastest-growing buyer segment in commercial landscaping (76% of deal volume). A single platform sale deploys Bobyard across 5–15 entities. The sales cycle is longer but the LTV is 10–20x a single contractor. And once Bobyard is the standard at one platform, it becomes the default for every future acquisition—built-in expansion.

**Recommendation 3: Trigger Outbound on Government RFP Drops, Not Just Firmographics**

> **Do:** Build automated monitoring of government procurement portals (BidPrime, SAM.gov) for landscape RFPs over $1M. When a large RFP drops in a metro, immediately outbound to the 10–20 commercial landscape contractors in that area with a message tied to that specific opportunity. "The City of Phoenix just posted a $4.2M parks landscape project. 47 drawing sheets, 3,200 plant specs. Your team will spend 40 hours on the takeoff. Want to do it in 4?"
> **Instead of:** Only targeting contractors based on static firmographic data (revenue, headcount, location) with generic outreach.
> **Because:** RFP-triggered outreach creates urgency that firmographic targeting cannot. The contractor has a specific deadline, a specific project, and a specific pain point right now. This is the difference between "you should probably look at this sometime" and "you need this before March 15th." It also demonstrates domain expertise that builds instant credibility.
